About Me

I am a doctoral researcher at Université de Technologie de Compiègne (UTC), LMAC Laboratory, supervised by Prof. Faker Ben Belgacem and Prof. Faten Jelassi. My research integrates topology optimization and PDE analysis with AI-driven modeling for thermal energy systems, including phase change materials (PCM) and Kapitza interface laws.

Previously, I obtained my Master's degree in Computational Mathematics from Northwestern Polytechnical University (Double First-Class/985 University), under the supervision of Academician Weihong Zhang, Prof. Manyu Xiao, and Prof. Piotr Breitkopf. I received my Bachelor's degree in Mathematics and Applied Mathematics from the same institution.

Doctoral Experience

Detailed doctoral progress reconstructed from my annual CSI thesis progress reports at UTC-LMAC.

Year 1
Foundation of TES modeling, heat-transfer analysis, Kapitza interface laws, and topology optimization

The first year established the mathematical and computational foundation of my PhD project on hybrid thermal energy storage systems. The broad objective was to improve heat transfer in phase-change-material (PCM) based TES systems by combining topology optimization, shape optimization, reduced-order modeling, and physics-informed deep learning.

Research work completed
  • Clarified the thesis objective: design hybrid TES media with thermally conductive enhancers that preserve PCM storage capacity while accelerating heat charging, storage, and especially heat extraction during short thermal cycles.
  • Started from steady-state heat conduction by neglecting the time derivative, derived the governing equation analytically, and used numerical verification to understand the spatial structure of the temperature field.
  • Extended the work to transient heat conduction, using Laplace transforms, hyperbolic-function analysis, and series expansion to handle temporal terms, parameter sensitivity, and spatio-temporal heat-flux evolution.
  • Implemented one-dimensional and two-dimensional transient heat-conduction simulations in FreeFEM++, building the first computational basis for later finite element and topology optimization studies.
  • Introduced radiative heat-transfer effects through the Stefan-Boltzmann law, leading to nonlinear PDE systems and motivating the study of nonlinear thermal behavior.
  • Studied steady heat conduction in a double-layer domain with nonlinear Kapitza contact resistance at the interface, where imperfect contact creates resistance and slows heat transfer.
  • Used a simple one-dimensional model to demonstrate non-uniqueness of solutions under nonlinear Kapitza interface laws.
  • Applied Picard iteration to compute fixed points of the associated algebraic mapping and used bifurcation analysis with the conductivity ratio of two layers as a control parameter.
  • Observed several bifurcation mechanisms for power-law Kapitza conductivities, including transcritical bifurcation, pitchfork bifurcation, flip bifurcation, and saddle-node bifurcation.
  • Studied the theory of topological derivatives and carried out derivations using the Dirichlet-to-Neumann operator.
  • Implemented an inverse/topology optimization procedure in FreeFEM++ for two-dimensional transient heat conduction, connecting thermal sensitivity analysis with material-layout design.
Training and academic activities
  • Completed doctoral training in practical problem-solving tools, random modeling methods, French language training, scientific Python and JupyterLab, and the CIMPA research school on Mathematics, Artificial Intelligence and Applications.
  • Participated in FreeFEM Days in Paris, the ANR NumOpTES project meeting at the University of Bordeaux, a winter school on control, optimization, and artificial intelligence for machine learning in Tunisia, and the LMAC monthly doctoral and postdoctoral seminar.
Planned continuation after Year 1
  • Build a full one-dimensional bifurcation atlas for scalar Kapitza models, including codimension-one continuation, period-k cascades, and multi-parameter unfoldings in the exponent, boundary data, and coefficients.
  • Develop nonlinear elliptic and parabolic heat-transfer models with volumetric sources, state-dependent coefficients, and nonlinear interface or boundary laws.
  • Study existence, uniqueness, global attractors, basins of attraction, and feedback or optimal-control strategies for transient nonlinear heat-transfer models.
  • Construct reduced-order modeling strategies based on PINNs, using physical constraints to reduce labeled-data requirements while improving scalability and interpretability.
Year 2
Consolidation around Kapitza interface FEM, continuation methods, topological derivatives, and XPINN solvers

The second year made the thesis direction more coherent and technically focused. The work developed along three connected lines: numerical Kapitza interface problems, topology optimization through topological derivatives, and physics-informed domain-decomposition solvers for heterogeneous heat conduction.

Research work completed
  • Refined the thesis framework around heat-transfer models for PCMs and thermal storage systems, emphasizing heterogeneous media, material interfaces, inclusions, phase boundaries, and imperfect thermal contact.
  • Identified Kapitza-type interface conditions as the central mathematical mechanism for modeling temperature jumps and thermal resistance across composite interfaces.
  • Developed and tested computational tools for both linear and nonlinear Kapitza interface laws, using FreeFEM and Matlab for simulation, post-processing, and parameter studies.
  • Investigated nonlinear interface conditions numerically and found that standard Newton iterations and classical parameter continuation may fail or become unstable when multiple branches or turning points occur.
  • Used pseudo-arclength continuation as a more robust strategy to follow nonlinear solution branches through folds and to recover branches that direct continuation may miss.
  • Analyzed branch selection, sensitivity to initial guesses, convergence difficulty, non-uniqueness, and bifurcation-type behavior caused by nonlinear Kapitza laws.
  • Continued the study of topological derivatives for heat-transfer and topology optimization problems, focusing on how small inclusions or material perturbations change thermal objective functionals.
  • Implemented and verified topological-derivative-based criteria for identifying favorable regions for material modification or inclusion insertion.
  • Developed XPINN-based solvers for steady variable-coefficient heat-conduction equations, using domain decomposition to handle subdomains with different thermal conductivities.
  • Extended XPINN implementation to time-dependent variable-coefficient heat-conduction equations, creating a preliminary physics-informed framework for heterogeneous thermal media.
  • Used XPINN experiments to assess how neural domain-decomposition methods could later support Kapitza interface modeling, topology optimization, and repeated parametric simulations.
  • Consolidated the theoretical background of the linear Kapitza problem, including weak formulation, the role of interface terms, and finite element discretization.
Scientific outputs
  • Published the work on steady conduction problems with nonlinear Kapitza contact resistance in Mathematics and Computers in Simulation, focusing on existence and non-uniqueness.
  • Prepared and deposited the HAL preprint on semilinear elliptic problems with nonlinear contact resistance in composite domains.
  • Prepared a May 2026 research presentation connecting Kapitza bifurcations, probabilistic completeness ideas for multi-solution discovery, and XPINN experiments.
Training and planned continuation
  • Added doctoral training in Advanced Mechanics of Solids, Fluids, Structures and Flexible Multibody Systems, complementing earlier training in modeling, scientific Python, language, and AI-oriented mathematics.
  • Set the next thesis objectives: strengthen the linear FEM formulation of Kapitza problems, systematically analyze nonlinear interface laws through continuation, and develop topology/shape-derivative-based optimization for thermal storage systems.
  • Clarified the role of PINN and XPINN methods as complementary tools rather than replacements for finite element analysis, with a focus on surrogate modeling, heterogeneous media, interface conditions, and optimization-driven simulations.
  • Defined the next publication directions: a rigorous chapter or article on linear Kapitza FEM formulation and implementation, followed by nonlinear interface laws, bifurcation phenomena, and optimization frameworks for thermal storage applications.
